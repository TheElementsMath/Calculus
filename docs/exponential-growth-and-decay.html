3.8 Exponential Growth and Decay

Many real-world quantities grow or decay at a rate proportional to their size.

This means:

  • large quantities change faster than small ones
  • growth/decay accelerates as size increases
  • the process is self-reinforcing

Examples include:

  • population growth
  • radioactive decay
  • chemical reactions
  • cooling and heating
  • bacterial growth
  • continuously compounded interest
  • investment growth

3.8.1 The Law of Natural Growth and Decay

If \(y(t)\) is a quantity that changes at a rate proportional to its size, then: \[ \frac{dy}{dt} = ky \]

where:

  • \(k\) is a constant
  • \(k > 0\) → growth
  • \(k < 0\) → decay

This is a differential equation because it relates a function to its derivative.

Theorem 3.12 The only solutions of \[ \frac{dy}{dt} = ky \] are exponential functions: \[ y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}, \]

where:

  • \(y_0 = y(0)\) is the initial value
  • \(k\) is the relative growth/decay rate

From \[ \frac{dP}{dt} = kP, \] divide both sides by \(P\): \[ \frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dt} = k, \] so \[ \ln(P) = kt \Longrightarrow P = e^{kt}.\]

Interpretation:

  • \(k\) is the relative growth rate
  • percentage change per unit time
  • constant proportional growth

3.8.2 Population Growth Model

If \(P_0\) is the initial population and \(k\) is the growth rate, the population is given by: \[ P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \]

Example 3.47 Population Model

Let \[ P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \] with

  • Initial population: \(P_0 = 1000\)
  • Growth rate: \(k = 0.03\) (3% per year)

Then, \[ P(t) = 1000e^{0.03t} \]

If we look at the population after 10 years, \[ P(10) = 1000e^{0.3} \approx 1349 \]

Radioactive Decay:

Decay also follows: \[ m(t) = m_0 e^{kt}, \quad k<0 \]

Half-Life Model:

If \(h\) is the half-life: \[ m(t) = m_0\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/h} \]

Example 3.48 Half-Life

If the initial mass is \(m_0 = 80\) g, and half-life is \(h = 5\) years, find the mass in 10 years and the time it takes to reach 10 g.

Solution:

\[ m(t) = 80\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/5} \] Mass after 10 years: \[ m(10) = 80\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = 20 \text{ g} \]

Time to reach 10 g: \[ 80\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/5} = 10 \] \[ \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/5} = \frac{1}{8} \Rightarrow t=15 \text{ years} \]

3.8.3 Newton’s Law of Cooling

Newton’s law of cooling is \[ \frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_s) \] where:

  • \(T(t)\) = object temperature
  • \(T_s\) = surrounding temperature
  • \(k\) = a constant

Solution: \[ T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)e^{kt} \]

Example 3.49 Cooling

Suppose the room temperature is \(T_s = 20^\circ C\) and the initial temperature: \(T_0 = 90^\circ C\).
Find the temperature after 10 minutes if \(k = -0.1\).

Solution:

\[ T(t) = 20 + 70e^{-0.1t} \]

Temperature after 10 minutes: \[ T(10) = 20 + 70e^{-1} \approx 45.8^\circ C \]

3.8.4 Continuously Compounded Interest

\[ A(t) = A_0 e^{rt} \]

where:

  • \(A_0\) = initial investment
  • \(r\) = interest rate
  • \(t\) = time

Example 3.50 Investment Growth

Initial investment: \(A_0 = 1000\). Interest rate: \(r = 0.05\). How much do we have after 10 years.

Solution:

\[ A(t) = 1000e^{0.05t} \] Value after 10 years: \[ A(10) = 1000e^{0.5} \approx 1649 \]

3.8.5 Putting It All Together

All of the following follow the same structure: \[ \frac{dy}{dt} = ky \] \[ y(t) = y_0 e^{kt} \]

Context Meaning of \(y\)
Population number of organisms
Finance account balance
Physics radioactive mass
Chemistry concentration
Biology bacteria count
Thermodynamics temperature difference
Economics capital growth

Different meanings — same mathematics.

3.8.5.1 Conceptual Takeaways

  • Proportional change leads to exponential behavior
  • Growth and decay use the same equation
  • The sign of \(k\) determines growth vs decay
  • The initial value controls scaling
  • Exponential change is nonlinear
  • Small growth rates produce large long-term effects
  • Relative growth rate is constant in these models
