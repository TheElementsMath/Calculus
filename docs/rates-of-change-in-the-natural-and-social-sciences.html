3.7 Rates of Change in the Natural and Social Sciences

In calculus, the derivative represents rate of change. If
\[ y = f(x) \] then the derivative
\[ \frac{dy}{dx} \] represents the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).

This single mathematical idea appears across:

  • physics
  • chemistry
  • biology
  • economics
  • engineering
  • social sciences

If \(x\) changes from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\), then: \[ \Delta x = x_2 - x_1 \] \[ \Delta y = f(x_2) - f(x_1) \]

The average rate of change is: \[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} \]

Interpretation:

  • Slope of the secant line
  • Average change over an interval
  • Mean behavior over time/space

The instantaneous rate of change is the limit of the average rate of change: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \]

Interpretation:

  • Slope of the tangent line
  • Rate of change at a single point
  • Local behavior of a function

3.7.1 Physics Applications

Let:

  • \(s(t)\) = position
  • \(v(t) = \frac{ds}{dt}\) = velocity
  • \(a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}\) = acceleration

Relationships: \[ v(t) = s'(t) \] \[ a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) \]

Example 3.42 Motion of a Particle

Find the velocity and acceleration. \[ s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t. \] Determine the velocity at \(t = 2\) and when the particle is at rest.

Solution:

  1. Velocity: \[ v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 \]

  2. Acceleration: \[ a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12 \]

  3. Velocity at \(t=2\): \[ v(2) = 3(4) - 12(2) + 9 = 12 - 24 + 9 = -3 \]

  4. Particle at rest: \[ v(t)=0 \Rightarrow 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 0 \] \[ t=1,\; t=3 \]

Interpretation:

  • derivative = velocity
  • second derivative = acceleration
  • zeros of velocity = rest points
  • sign of velocity = direction of motion

3.7.2 Physics: Density and Current

If \(m(x)\) = mass up to position \(x\), then: \[ \rho(x) = \frac{dm}{dx}. \] This is mass per unit length.

Example 3.43 Density of a Rod

Let \[ m(x) = x^2. \] Find \(\rho(x)\) and \(\rho(1)\).

Solution:

\[ \rho(x) = m'(x) = 2x \]

At \(x=1\): \[ \rho(1) = 2 \text{ kg/m} \]

3.7.3 Electric Current

If \(Q(t)\) = charge, then: \[ I(t) = \frac{dQ}{dt}. \] This is charge flow per unit time.

3.7.4 Chemistry Applications

If \(C(t)\) = concentration of a substance: \[ \text{Rate of reaction} = \frac{dC}{dt} \]

Interpretation:

  • products: positive derivative
  • reactants: negative derivative

Example 3.44 Reaction Rate

If: \[ C(t) = 5t^2. \] Find the rate of reaction at \(t=2\).

Solution: \[ \frac{dC}{dt} = 10t. \]

At \(t=2\): \[ \frac{dC}{dt} = 20 \text{ units/time} \]

3.7.5 Biology Applications

If \(n(t)\) = population size: \[ \text{Growth rate} = \frac{dn}{dt} \]

Example 3.45 Exponential Growth

Find the growth rate at \(t = 4\) if \[ n(t) = 100\cdot 2^t \]

Solution \[ \frac{dn}{dt} = 100\cdot 2^t \ln 2. \] At \(t=4\): \[ \frac{dn}{dt} = 100(16)\ln2 \approx 1109 \text{ organisms/hour}. \]

3.7.6 Economics Applications

If \(C(x)\) = total cost: \[ \text{Marginal cost} = C'(x). \]

This approximates the cost of producing one more unit.

Example 3.46 Cost Function

Find the marginal cost and the cost of producing the \(501^{st}\) unit if: \[ C(x) = 10{,}000 + 5x + 0.01x^2. \]

Solution:

\[ C'(x) = 5 + 0.02x \]

At \(x=500\): \[ C'(500) = 15 \text{ dollars/item} \]

Cost of producing the next unit ≈ $15.

3.7.7 Putting It All Together

The derivative is a universal language of change.

Different sciences interpret it differently, but mathematically it is always: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} \]

Across all disciplines:

Field Meaning of Derivative
Physics velocity, acceleration
Chemistry reaction rate
Biology population growth
Economics marginal cost
Engineering flow rates
Medicine blood flow gradient
Sociology spread of information
Psychology learning rate

Different meanings — same mathematics.

3.7.7.1 Conceptual Takeaways

  • A derivative is always a rate
  • Average rate of change → secant slope
  • Instantaneous rate of change → tangent slope
  • Units matter:
    \[ \frac{\text{units of } y}{\text{units of } x} \]
  • Every derivative has a physical interpretation
  • Graphs encode rates through slopes
  • Derivatives unify many scientific ideas
  • Calculus translates real-world change into mathematics
