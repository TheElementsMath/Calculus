2.7 Derivatives and Rates of Change

The problems of finding the tangent line to a curve and finding the velocity of a moving object both involve the same special type of limit.
This type of limit is called a derivative.

Derivatives are fundamental because they represent:

  • Slopes of curves
  • Velocities of moving objects
  • Rates of change in science, engineering, economics, biology, and social sciences

2.7.1 Tangent Lines and Limits

Let a curve \(C\) be defined by: \[ y = f(x) \] and let \(P(a, f(a))\) be a point on the curve. Choose a nearby point \(Q(x, f(x))\), where \(x \neq a\).

The slope of the secant line \(PQ\) is: \[ m_{PQ} = \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}. \] As \(Q \to P\) (that is, as \(x \to a\)), the secant line approaches the tangent line.

Definition 2.19 The tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at the point \((a, f(a))\) is the line through \((a, f(a))\) with slope: \[ m = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}, \] if this limit exists.

Definition 2.20 Let: \[ h = x - a \Rightarrow x = a + h. \] Then the slope becomes: \[ m = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}. \] This form is often easier to compute.

Example 2.32 Tangent Line via Definition

Find the tangent line to \(y = x^2\) at \((1,1)\).

Solution:

\[ m = \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \]

Factor:

\[ = \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} \]

\[ = \lim_{x \to 1} (x+1) = 2 \]

Slope \(m = 2\)

Equation (point-slope form):

\[ y - 1 = 2(x - 1) \]

\[ y = 2x - 1 \]

2.7.2 Derivatives

Because this type of limit appears everywhere, it is given a special name.

Definition 2.21 The derivative of \(f\) at \(a\), denoted \(f'(a)\), is: \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}. \] Equivalently: \[ f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}. \]

Geometrically, we’ve seen that \(f^{\prime}\) represents the slope of a tangent line to the graph of \(f\). \[ f'(a) = \text{slope of the tangent line to } y=f(x) \text{ at } x=a \]

If \(s = f(t)\) is the position function of an object: \[ \text{Average velocity} = \frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h} \] Taking the limit: \[ v(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(t+h)-f(t)}{h} \] So, the velocity of an object is: \[ v(t) = f'(t) \]

Example 2.33 Velocity from Position

Let: \[ s(t) = 4.9t^2. \] Find the velocity.

Solution:

Compute velocity: \[ v(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(t+h)^2 - 4.9t^2}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(t^2 + 2th + h^2 - t^2)}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(2th + h^2)}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} 4.9(2t + h) \] \[ = 9.8t \]

So:

\[ v(t) = 9.8t. \]

Example 2.34 Derivative at a Point

Let: \[ f(x) = x^2 - 8x + 9 \] Find \(f'(a)\).

Solution: \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(a+h)^2 - 8(a+h) + 9 - (a^2 - 8a + 9)}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2ah + h^2 - 8h}{h} \] \[ = \lim_{h \to 0} (2a + h - 8) \] \[ = 2a - 8. \]

So: \[ f'(a) = 2a - 8. \]

2.7.3 Tangent Line Using Derivatives

The equation of the tangent line at \((a, f(a))\) is:

\[ y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a) \]

Example 2.35 Tangent Line Using Derivative

Let: \[ f(x) = x^2 - 8x + 9 \] Find the tangent line to \(f\) at \(x=3\).

Solution:

\[ f'(x) = 2x - 8 \Rightarrow f'(3) = -2 \] At the point \(x = 3\), \[ f(3) = 9 - 24 + 9 = -6 \] Therefore, the equation is \[ y + 6 = -2(x - 3) \] or \[ y = -2x. \]

2.7.4 Rates of Change

If \(y = f(x)\), then:

  • Average rate of change: \[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} \]

  • Instantaneous rate of change: \[ \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = f'(x) \]

The derivative represents:

  • Slope of tangent line
  • Velocity (physics)
  • Marginal cost (economics)
  • Growth rate (biology)
  • Reaction rate (chemistry)
  • Power (physics)
  • Population growth
  • Debt growth
  • Production efficiency
  • Learning curves

Example 2.36 Real-World Interpretation

Let \(C(x)\) be the cost (in dollars) to produce \(x\) units.

Then: \[ C'(x) = \text{marginal cost} \] Units: \[ \frac{\text{dollars}}{\text{unit}} \]

If: \[ C'(1000) = 9 \]

Then, the cost of producing the next unit at 1000 units is approximately $9.

2.7.5 Putting It All Together

2.7.5.1 Conceptual Takeaways

  • Derivatives arise from limits of secant slopes
  • Tangent lines are limiting positions of secant lines
  • Derivatives unify geometry and motion
  • Derivatives measure local behavior
  • Zooming in makes curves look linear
  • Velocity is a derivative
  • Rates of change are derivatives
  • Tangents and rates of change are the same mathematically
  • Derivatives apply across disciplines
  • Slopes encode physical meaning
  • Units of derivatives matter
  • Local linearity is fundamental
  • Calculus connects algebra, geometry, and motion

2.7.5.2 Skills You Should Be Able To Do

You should now be able to:

  • Define a derivative using limits
  • Compute derivatives from first principles
  • Interpret derivatives geometrically
  • Interpret derivatives physically
  • Find tangent line equations
  • Compute slopes of tangent lines
  • Use point-slope form correctly
  • Model velocity using derivatives
  • Interpret units of derivatives
  • Explain rates of change
  • Distinguish average vs instantaneous change
  • Apply derivatives to real-world contexts
  • Connect graphs to derivatives
  • Understand local linearization
  • Use both \(h \to 0\) and \(x \to a\) definitions
  • Explain the meaning of a derivative in words
  • Interpret derivative values contextually
