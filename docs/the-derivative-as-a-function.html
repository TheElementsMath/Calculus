2.8 The Derivative as a Function

Previously, we defined the derivative of a function \(f\) at a fixed number \(a\) by \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}. \] Now we allow the point \(a\) to vary and replace it with a variable \(x\). This gives the derivative as a function: \[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}. \]

This means:

  • The derivative is not just a number at one point.
  • The derivative is a new function that assigns a slope value to every input \(x\) where the limit exists.

For each value of \(x\) we can interpret \(f'(x)\) as the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((x,f(x))\). Geometrically, the graph of \(f'\) records the slopes of \(f\).

Example 2.37 Polynomial Function

Problem:
Let \(f(x) = x^3 - x\). Find \(f'(x)\).

Solution:

\[\begin{align*} f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^3 - (x+h) - (x^3 - x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3 - x - h - x^3 + x}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3 - h}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left( 3x^2 + 3xh + h^2 - 1 \right) \\ &= 3x^2 - 1. \end{align*}\]

We can interpret the slope of \(f\) in terms of increasing and decreasing:

  • Where \(f'(x) > 0\): function increasing
  • Where \(f'(x) < 0\): function decreasing
  • Where \(f'(x) = 0\): horizontal tangent lines

Example 2.38 Square Root Function and Domain of the Derivative

Problem:
Let \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\). Find \(f'(x)\) and its domain.

Solution:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h} \]

Multiply by the conjugate: \[\begin{align*} f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}\\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x})(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} \\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} \\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} \\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}} \\ & = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}. \end{align*}\]

Domain:
Since division by \(\sqrt{x}\) requires \(x>0\) (because we take roots of positive numbers and we cannot divide by 0), \[ \text{Domain of } f'(x): (0,\infty) \]

Definition 2.22 A function \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\) if \(f'(a)\) exists. It is differentiable on an interval if the derivative exists at every point in that interval.

Example 2.39 Absolute Value Function

Let \(f(x) = |x|\). Determine where \(f\) is differentiable.

Solution:

Piecewise form: \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x, & x>0 \\ -x, & x<0 \end{cases} \]

Derivative: \[ f'(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & x>0 \\ -1, & x<0 \end{cases} \] At \(x=0\):

  • Left-hand limit \(\to -1\)
  • Right-hand limit \(\to 1\)

Since the limits are not equal: \[ f'(0) \text{ does not exist}. \] So \(f(x)=|x|\) is not differentiable at \(x=0\).

2.8.1 Continuity vs Differentiability

Definition 2.23 If \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\), then \(f\) is continuous at \(a\).

Important: The converse is false:

A function can be continuous but not differentiable.

How a Function Can Fail to Be Differentiable?

A function is not differentiable at a point if:

  1. Corner / kink (e.g. \(|x|\) at \(x=0\))
  2. Discontinuity (jump, hole, asymptote)
  3. Vertical tangent (slope becomes infinite)

2.8.2 Higher Derivatives

If \(f\) is differentiable, then its derivative \(f'\) is also a function and may itself be differentiable.

  • First derivative:
    \[ f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} \]

  • Second derivative:
    \[ f''(x) = \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} \]

  • Third derivative: \[ f'''(x) = \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3} \]

  • \(n\)-th derivative: \[ f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} \]

Example 2.40 Second Derivative

Let \(f(x) = x^3 - x\). Find the second derivative of \(f\).

Solution:

\[ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 1 \] \[ f''(x) = 6x \]

We can interpret derivatives in the following way:

  • \(f'(x)\): slope of the curve
  • \(f''(x)\): rate of change of the slope
  • \(f''(x) > 0\): slope increasing
  • \(f''(x) < 0\): slope decreasing

Physically, if \(s(t)\) is the position of a function:

  • Velocity:
    \[ v(t) = s'(t) \]
  • Acceleration:
    \[ a(t) = s''(t) \]
  • Jerk: \[ j(t) = s'''(t) \]

Each derivative represents a rate of change of the previous quantity.

2.8.3 Putting It All Together

The derivative transforms a function into a new function that describes change. It unifies geometry (slopes), physics (motion), economics (marginal change), and data science (rates, trends, dynamics) into one mathematical concept:

The derivative is the mathematics of change itself.

2.8.3.1 Conceptual Takeaways

  • The derivative is a function, not just a number.
  • \(f'(x)\) represents the slope of the tangent line at every point.
  • The derivative encodes how the function changes.
  • Differentiability implies continuity.
  • A function may be continuous but not differentiable.
  • Higher derivatives measure changes of changes:
    • slope → curvature → acceleration → jerk → etc.

2.8.3.2 Skills You Should Be Able To Do

By the end of this section, you should be able to:

  • Compute derivatives using the limit definition
  • Interpret \(f'(x)\) geometrically
  • Interpret \(f'(x)\) physically (rate of change)
  • Identify where a function is:
    • differentiable
    • not differentiable
  • Explain why differentiability implies continuity
  • Find second and higher derivatives
  • Interpret second derivatives conceptually
  • Connect:
    • \(f\) → position
    • \(f'\) → velocity
    • \(f''\) → acceleration
    • \(f'''\) → jerk
